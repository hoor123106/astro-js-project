Citrus EOs are crucial across disciplines, spanning perfumes and aromatherapy to pharmaceuticals. Their global market, worth 6.31 billion USD in 2018, keeps steadily expanding.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="the-molecules">The molecules</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus oils, such as bergamot or sweet orange, are rich in small, highly volatile molecules. That’s why they evaporate fast, providing citrus scents with their signature quick, uplifting burst while making them dissipate faster than heavier scents. These molecules engage our olfactory system, eliciting reactions both somatic and psychological.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Take citrus, for instance, where research indicates that sniffing orange essential oil reduces oxyhemoglobin in the brain, resulting in calm and comfort. <strong>Bergamot essential oil</strong> is associated with less anxiety and improved mood.</p><p class="pm-align--left">The main aromatic molecules that make up these oils include:</p><ul class=""><li>Limonene is dominant in lemon, orange, and grapefruit and brings a sharp, zesty edge.</li><li>Linalool: found in bergamot, provides a soft, floral tone.</li><li>Citral: gives lemon and lime their distinct, bright aroma.</li><li>Gamma-terpinene: adds a subtle, sweet undertone.</li><li>Beta-pinene: offers a fresh, woody nuance.</li></ul><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus EOs are complex, composed of approximately 400 different compounds, all of which contribute to its scent and body interactions.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="the-species">The species</h3><p class="pm-align--left">There are several key citrus species used in fragrance: bergamot, sweet orange, lemon, grapefruit, and mandarin. <h2 class="pm-align--left" id="capturing-the-fresh-aroma">Capturing the fresh aroma</h2><p class="pm-align--left">Lemon and orange, in particular, are known for their refreshing aromas and brightening properties, which is why they’re used so often as the head notes of a fragrance. These fragrances are distinctive in their clean and bright character and are a staple of many summer and daytime scents. There’s something about the craft of bottling up these fragrances that merges heritage, research, and an appreciation for the rhythms of the earth.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="1-cold-pressing">1. Cold pressing</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Cold pressing is an age-old means of extracting citrus peel oils. It’s basic. The rinds are crushed, on occasion by means of machines, to extract the aromatic oils. Unlike heat-based methods, cold pressing uses no high temperatures, so the pure, sharp aroma of the fruit remains intact.</p><p class="pm-align--left">This is important because citrus oils are fragile. Heat can alter their fragrance, at times making them less fresh. Cold pressing imparts you oils with those same lively, zesty notes you encounter when hand-peeling an orange or lemon. Oranges, lemons, limes, and bergamots are best for this.</p><p class="pm-align--left">It preserves the oils’ effervescence, so what you smell in a bottle is right near what you’d sniff on a sun-baked citrus grove morning.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="2-distillation">2. Distillation</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Distillation, particularly steam distillation, is another common method to obtain citrus scents. Here, steam passes through the fruit peels, absorbing oil along the way. It’s then cooled, and the oil is skimmed.</p><p class="pm-align--left">It can alter the oil’s character, mellowing the scent or eliminating certain pungent notes. Distillation is efficient and can extract fragrances that are more difficult to obtain via cold pressing. It’s frequently used on oils such as petitgrain, which is derived from citrus leaves and twigs instead of solely from peels.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Although not always as fruit-true as cold press, distillation is prized for its ability to produce more stable oils for perfumery.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="3-terroirs-touch">3. Terroir's touch</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Where the citrus is grown means a lot. Soil, climate, and the way the sun hits the trees influence the scent. Citrus from different regions, like southern Italy or some areas of South America, can smell different.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Certain areas lend the fruits sweeter, more floral oils, while others impart a more tart or spicy scent. Bergamot, for example, from Calabria, Italy, is prized for its unique profile: a mix of citrus brightness and a gentle floral spice.</p><p class="pm-align--left">In the luxury market, it’s these nuances that distinguish top scents and give them a location and a narrative.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="4-harvest-time">4. Harvest time</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Everything changes when citrus is picked. Harvested at their peak, these fruits contain the richest, most complex oils. If picked too soon, the scent could be brash or too pungent. If picked too late, the aroma can turn flat.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Seasonal shifts, such as rain or chilly evenings, influence the resulting aroma. <strong>Timing is everything</strong> when it comes to scent catching, particularly for premium perfumes, which tend to depend on the freshest, most vibrant notes.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Perfumers track the weather, check ripeness, and at times, smell-test it right in the grove to capture that <strong>fresh aroma</strong>.</p><h2 class="pm-align--left" id="a-global-history-of-citrus">A global history of citrus</h2><p class="pm-align--left"><img src="https://storage.googleapis.com/surgegraph-ai-images/txt2img_4jlbGTAeD1_de_a-global-history-of-citrus.jpg" sg-media="" /></p><p class="pm-align--left">The story of citrus begins millennia ago with a mere handful of wild species in Southeast Asia. Eventually, these fruits spread around the world, transported by merchants, invaders, and adventurers. Citrus has shaped literature, art, medicine, and trade, linking people across the world and imprinting distinct cultures.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Wherever citrus journeyed, it was a symbol of renewal and vigor, its fragrance interlaced with practice and lifestyle.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="ancient-rituals">Ancient rituals</h3><p class="pm-align--left">In the ancient world, we ritualized citrus for its fragrant brightness and brilliance. The citron, for instance, was the earliest citrus fruit to arrive in the West, found by Alexander the Great’s soldiers as they swept through the Persian Empire. Many thought citrus was able to keep evil away and attract positive energies, so they were integrated into various religious rituals and celebrations.</p><p class="pm-align--left">These ancient traditions leaned towards using citrus to purify rooms or denote significance, and this aura of protection and cleansing lingers still today in modern wellness practices. Whether whole or as oils, citrus fruits continue to feature in spiritual rituals, from temple offerings in Asia to scented candles used for meditation.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="traditional-wellness">Traditional wellness</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus had been prized in traditional medicine. Medieval herbalists began to look to citrus for its <strong>healing prowess</strong>. Citrus essential oils were used to treat and purify the air in sick rooms.</p><p class="pm-align--left">We all know the tale of scurvy and sailors—it was the need to prevent this deadly disease that kept British ships stocked with lemons and limes during the Napoleonic wars, which in turn created the moniker ‘Limeys.’ Today, citrus notes appear in aromatherapy, employed to improve moods and reduce anxiety.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Around the world, people continue to rely on citrus for its <strong>wellness properties</strong>—mixing ancient wisdom with modern science.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="culinary-arts">Culinary arts</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus influences the way we cook and eat in so many parts of the world. Its zesty, refreshing aroma lends vibrancy to recipes, slicing through rich flavors and grounding sweetness. Chefs employ lemon zest, orange peel, and yuzu in sauces, desserts, and drinks for both flavor and scent.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus oils add a richer dimension, rendering food more appetizing. Many of our great classic cuisines—from Mediterranean to East Asian—rely on citrus to characterize them. That connection between citrus food and perfumery is a potent one.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Both capitalize on the fruit’s power to invigorate our senses and imprint memories.</p><h2 class="pm-align--left" id="the-feeling-of-bergamot-perfume">The feeling of bergamot perfume</h2><p class="pm-align--left"><img src="https://storage.googleapis.com/surgegraph-ai-images/txt2img__-qWSFI563_de_the-feeling-of-bergamot-perfume.jpg" sg-media="" /></p><p class="pm-align--left">Something about the zing of bergamot perfume. It’s frequently compared to the scent of a spring morning or summer sun, bergamot being neither too sweet nor floral and matching in between the effervescent brightness of an orange or lemon. It is one of the most common top notes used in perfumes, providing a warm yet fresh introductory burst.</p><p class="pm-align--left">These traits make bergamot not just enticing on a perfumer’s palette, but profoundly potent for mood and memory.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="emotional-response">Emotional response</h3><p class="pm-align--left">We all experience a cathartic mood-lift the moment we catch a whiff of bergamot. It’s a <strong>powerful sensory memory</strong> tool. For a lot of us, a well-known citrus note can remind us of childhood summers, family get-togethers, or significant milestones.</p><p class="pm-align--left">The connection between scent and memory is powerful. When you wear or sniff bergamot perfume, you can suddenly recall a place, a person, or an emotion from the past. These memories are often accompanied by a surge of emotion, demonstrating how intimately connected scent and memory are.</p><p class="pm-align--left"><strong>Citrus scents like bergamot</strong> keep bergamot perfume people making those moments and moments easier to remember. One spritz and suddenly the orange-tinged scent of bergamot perfume can open a window to the past or make recency just as vivid.</p><h2 class="pm-align--left" id="pairing-citrus-notes">Pairing citrus notes</h2><p class="pm-align--left"><img src="https://storage.googleapis.com/surgegraph-ai-images/txt2img_BNccvvqPU-_de_pairing-citrus-notes.jpg" sg-media="" /></p><p class="pm-align--left">Pairing the citrus notes with other scent families is a subtle art. Citrus is present throughout perfumery because it is at once both luminous and versatile. It can enhance an accord or contribute a tart, bright facet. Citrus marries well with nearly every family—from florals to woods to spices and aquatics—providing perfumers with countless avenues to construct layers and impart complexity.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="with-florals">With florals</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus notes highlight the best of florals by imparting sparkle and freshness to the blend. Citrus notes have a crispness that can cut through sweet or powdery florals, making the overall scent feel lighter and more modern. It works particularly well when paired with timeless florals such as jasmine and rose.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Jasmine’s sweet, full-bodied scent gets a breezy lift from lemon or bergamot. Rose, often deep and even heavy, is uplifted by the addition of orange or grapefruit.</p><ul class=""><li>Jasmine: Citrus makes jasmine less heady, more radiant.</li><li>Rose: Grapefruit or orange peels remove heaviness and add a playful layer.</li><li>Lily of the valley: Lemon or yuzu brings a clean, dew-like freshness.</li><li>Peony: Tangerine adds a soft, fruity brightness.</li></ul><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus-floral blends are so popular in <strong>contemporary perfumery</strong> because they seem romantic without being antiquated. They’re typically selected for daywear or spring/summer, but the combo has sufficient punch to endure all year.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="with-woods">With woods</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus and woods complement each other. Woods such as sandalwood and cedar are deep, earthy, and smooth. On their own, they can sometimes feel too heavy. Citrus slices through, providing a crisp top note that freshens the whole combination awake. It makes the perfume feel fresher and more wearable.</p><p class="pm-align--left"><strong>Citrus-wood blends</strong> are typically unisex for anyone who appreciates understated depth. These concoctions appear in a lot of <strong>luxury and specialty fragrances</strong>. Calabrian is frequently selected for its gentler, more floral nuance, while Tunisian adds a bracing, crisp quality.</p><p class="pm-align--left">They each pair beautifully with warm woods, which cause the scent to linger and develop as you wear it. Perfumes with this blend are wonderful for the cooler months, and lighter versions are good for summer.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="with-spices">With spices</h3><p class="pm-align--left">Citrus and spices prove to be a <strong>lively couple</strong>. The vibrancy of citrus enhances warm spices such as cardamom, clove, or cinnamon. It can be an audacious blend. Some people think citrus in spicy scents is a little ‘sharp’; others adore the wake-up factor.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Yuzu, that metallic note, just adds something so special and artistic when you’re paired with spice. Like citrus-spice blends, they’re good for cold weather. They’re warm but not too weighty. Adding vanilla or caramel to the mix brings a sweet, indulgent touch.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Balance is key. Too much citrus can overpower the spices, while just a touch adds brightness. Often paired with herbs in <strong>winter fragrances</strong>, this is a demonstration of how versatile citrus is.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="with-aquatics">With aquatics</h3><p class="pm-align--left">If you pair them with citrus notes, aquatics make scents that feel like a <strong>breath of fresh air</strong>. While the citrus market is already worth more than $10 billion globally, it’s only going to grow more valuable, with essential oils alone expected to hit $1 billion in 10 years. These changes impact everything from citrus agriculture to perfumery.</p><h3 class="pm-align--left" id="trends-shaping-the-future">Trends shaping the future</h3><ol><li><p class="pm-align--left"><strong>Sustainability as a core value:</strong> Fragrance brands are choosing responsibly sourced citrus, responding to consumer demand for ethical products. <h3 class="pm-align--left" id="sustainable-sourcing">Sustainable sourcing</h3><p class="pm-align--left">More growers employ eco-friendly techniques, such as water-conserving irrigation or organic pest management. Good practices maintain soil vitality and protect native species. Brands opting for these sources typically receive superior, more vibrant oils, which translates to a deeper aroma in the end product.</p><p class="pm-align--left">Groups worldwide, from smallholder co-ops to larger farms, are sharing knowledge and advocating for fair wages and transparency. Buyers have a part as well.
